Functions

A Broad Scope of Schedule items Brings Greater Flexibility

All you will need to describe the activities on your schedule are expressed by the following five notions:

Engagements

Confirmed meetings, lectures, and dentist appointments all fall into this category. Once saved, they fill the designated time slots and are shown in your schedule, unless they are deleted or canceled. When fitting recurring engagements in your schedule, you only have to find a free time slot for the first go. The unfitted ones will be put aside, and you may resolve the conflicts right away or leave them for later.

Happenings

Use happenings to follow events that interest you and plan your free time, setting them apart from your scheduled engagements. They are not put on your schedule when they are saved, and therefore you may save as many as you like within the same time frame. A simple tap can schedule a happening when you’re completely available and want to engage in it.

All-day Events

Think of them as the red circles you mark on a conventional calendar to indicate something special about a day or a certain period of time, such as birthdays, anniversaries, day-off, a two-week marketing campaign, etc. They are listed in the “All-day” section at the top of your schedule. However, they are not scheduled items and thus will not block the relevant time slots.

Tasks

Pay electricity bill, submit project proposal - obligations that you should or must fulfill by a specific time. Like engagements, tasks are shown in your schedule. However, multiple tasks can be scheduled to be due at the same time. Sometimes, you don’t have a specific time but a date for expected completion. For instance, you have a busy day but definitely want to give your mom a call when you finish work. You can set the due time as “day end”. The task will then be placed at the bottom of the timeline.

To-dos

Simply put, to-dos are tasks without a specific due time. You might want to try baking a pie or replace an outdated but functioning TV. These can probably wait and does not require day-to-day tracking and reminders being set up. If the TV all in a sudden broke, you could turn the to-do into a task and assign a due time to it in just a few taps.

 

 

A Collection of Versatile Tools to Meet Different Tastes and Needs.

SewUp offers different tools for different purposes. Each tool allows you to see your scheduled items in different ways according to, for example, time span, category, and status..

Schedule Planner

A time management tool. The default entry point for day-to-day management of your schedule, showing all active engagements and tasks on a condensed timeline with free time and happening indicators. Jump over days of the selected week in a tap or double-finger swipe. Scroll the week bar or call out the calendar for more flexible day selection.

Events Manager

An event planning tool. Three different views allows you to see and manage all-day events, engagements, and happenings respectively in day mode. You can instantly see your free time slots between engagements and quickly fit a new one in using the quick add buttons. Canceled engagements live in a separate container.

Tasks Manager

A task management tool. You can view all tasks (active, completed, on-hold) in daily, weekly, or monthly modes. Details of completed tasks are hidden by default and can be revealed instantly in a tap. You may also request a list of all records, which comes with your overall on-time completion rate. Alternatively, you can group tasks by categories, which is customizable.

SewUp thinks and speaks in the same manner as we do.

What you can act on schedule items matches how we handle them in real life. Other than creating, updating, and deleting, the following actions are non-trivial yet intuitive and handy:

  • Cancel engagement
  • Schedule tentative engagement
  • Complete task/to-do
  • Reactivate completed task/to-do
  • Put task on hold
  • Resume on-hold task
  • Turn to-do into task
